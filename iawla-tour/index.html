The Argument

Los Angeles has spent a century selling itself as a billboard. If I Awaken in Los Angeles is the show that walks behind the sign — through Boyle Heights and Koreatown, Leimert Park and Laurel Canyon, Filipinotown and the South Central church — and finds the city the postcards never printed. The real LA is not on the hill. It's in this room.

Director: Andrew Scoville · Composer: Derrick Hodge

Space 01 · Beat sequence

Hollywood Boulevard

01
Arrival
HOUSE UPTOURISTS GREET Audience enters from the lobby. The five dancers are already in the room, costumed as tourists — cameras around necks, fanny packs, maps, phones up. They warmly greet and direct the audience to find spots along the east wall. The theatrical convention is established without a word: we're all tourists here. Walk of Fame stars glow on the floor. LED wall holds a slow Hollywood Boulevard establishing shot.
02
Sekou enters
SPOTTCL CHINESE THEATREPHOTOS Spot finds the Tour Guide. "Welcome aboard, folks!" The wall pushes in on TCL Chinese Theatre — pagoda facade, cement handprints. Music: Hollywood Swinging. The tourist-dancers begin moving on the sidewalk — taking selfies, consulting maps, pointing at landmarks on the LED.
03
Landmarks cycle
DOLBY → WALK OF FAME → CAPITOL → HOLLYWOOD SIGNREACT As the Tour Guide narrates, the LED moves through landmark POVs. The tourists react in character — photographing the Hollywood Sign, miming shock at the stars underfoot, posing under Capitol Records, waving a friend into frame. The sidewalk is full of life.
04
The pivot
MELLOWSTREET-LEVEL FACESSLOW Music softens. Tour Guide: "I want you to look deeper." Landmarks recede. The LED lingers on real street-level faces — the fruit vendor, the auditioning woman. The tourists slow their motion. Cameras drop. Maps fold. They start actually looking.
05
The descent
PAVEMENT → SOIL → ROOTSSTARS FADE "Past the pavement, past the glamour, down into the very soil." LED camera pushes down through the sidewalk. Concrete cracks. Earth. Roots. Walk of Fame stars dim underfoot. The tourists stand still, arrested.
06
Blackout
BLACKOUTDARK LED wall goes black. Floor goes dark. The room holds a beat of silence. The tour is over.
07
The number
LIGHTS UPLINE · DANCEVIDEOAUDIO OVER SPEAKERS Lights rise. The five tourist-dancers are revealed in a line, spread along the sidewalk between the audience and the LED wall — poised, ready. A video plays on the wall; the song plays over the room's speakers. The dancers perform in front of the wall — no live poets, no live band in this space, just video, audio, and movement. The wall is the set; the dancers are the story.
08
Transition to Tunnel
WASHPATH LIT The Tour Guide returns. "Alright folks! Follow me…" A lit path on the floor draws the audience out of the room to the left, through a transition tunnel, and into the main show space. The room empties. The LED wall goes dark and stays dark until the audience returns at the very end.

The audience turns to see her. She and the Tour Guide volley for a moment — her sharp, him sheepish. Finally he cedes the stage and exits.

The band softens under her. She begins her poem. The two billboards flanking her come alive with projected images of the places she names — the 7-Eleven on Osborne, Mariachi Square, Van Nuys Blvd, Reseda, Sylmar, the 210 freeway curving through the hills. Audience faces north, transfixed.
07

08

From the other side of the room — from the mural wall behind them — a new voice: "Los Angeles is a tianguis, a marvelous marketplace…" The audience turns 180°. A follow spot finds the Elder Poet at the base of the mural.
09
The elder walks in
Elder walks through spaceLights chase elements
The Elder begins walking forward through the scenic elements as he names them. Lighting chases his path — the lowrider lights up when he speaks of "lowriding is where cars become moving museums"; the tianguis stalls glow when he says "where wisdom, song, and teachings go with the tamales"; the hanging merchandise catches color on "art is alive on the walls on skin and cars." The room is his illustration. His walk carries him steadily toward the Courtyard.
10
Final words in the Courtyard
Audience partsYouth descends · Elder finishes
The Elder reaches the edge of the crowd. The audience parts and he walks into the middle of the Courtyard. The Youth Poet steps down from the band stage and joins him there. He delivers the final lines of his poem from the center of the crowd — surrounded by the audience, Youth Poet beside him. The section ends with both poets standing floor-level in the middle of the Courtyard. Blackout holds briefly before the transition.

The exit is the passage through the Thai Town landmark — whatever form the writer chooses, the structure frames the audience's passage out into Space 4 (Black LA). Entered through one town, exited through another — the whole section lived inside a shrunken Asian-American LA. The five dancers remain posed on their stages as the audience passes; lights hold on the arc; they don't leave.
Space 04 · Beat sequence

Black LA across a century

01
Enter the church
Tour Guide liveProcession from Space 3
As the five dancers hold their last pose in Space 3, the Tour Guide returns to the audience — hyped, winded, grinning: "Whew! Now THAT's what I call a show — five towns, one breath, somebody hand me a glass of water!" He gathers them and starts walking east. "But we've still got ground to cover. Come with me this way…" As they cross into Space 4, his voice drops into something more reverent, more building, and he lands the line that sets the whole next section: "We're going to take you to church."

Audience faces west. The full church set is waiting — stage, altar rail, a soft tungsten wash. The LED screen is dark. The Tour Guide, gentler now, invites them in: "Alright, alright. There are some seats in the front for anyone who needs to sit down for a moment. For everyone else, gather around. Service is just about to get started…"
02
The choir · "I Know It Was The Blood"
LED live · warm church washChoir audio over speakers
The LED screen lights up with the choir video (or on special nights, the live 5–8 person choir takes the stage). Audio fills the room. "One day when I was lost, he died upon the cross / And I know it was the blood for me." The audience stands in reverence inside the boundary of pews, altar rail, and candles.
03
Dancers join · the church comes alive
5 dancers on church stageCelebratory break
As the choir hits its closing crescendo, the five dancers emerge from the wings onto the church stage. A brief, joyful dance break — call-and-response movement with the choir video, hands to the sky, gospel energy. Audience hands clap, some sway.
04
The pivot · poet's opening · call to follow
Church blackoutHeld silence → guitarPoet's opening stanzasAudience turns 180°
Lights cut on the church. Full blackout on the west wall. A single spotlight snaps up on the band stage to the east — and there, revealed, stands the Poet. The audience turns 180° as one. She delivers the first stanzas — "Welcome back, to the City of Angels! / Where we got equal angles / The hood an isosceles / Half light / Half dark / Gangstas spit wisdom like Socrates…" — still holding the audience in the church zone, letting them find her voice.

She lands the invitation: "…it seems I have your ear for a while. So let me tell you how our culture made this town." She pauses. The band's guitarist rolls in with a slow, rooted Leadbelly-style progression. The Tour Guide steps forward beside the dancers and picks up the thread: "Come on, y'all — she's about to take us back. Follow us, right this way…" The dancers offer their hands. The journey east begins.
05
Zone 1 · Migration · dancers usher the audience through
Poet from band stageZone 1 lights up · sepia amberDancers lead audience to zone
As the audience crosses into Zone 1, the Poet picks back up — "In 1865, The 13th Amendment freed the slaves… 6 million freemen and women set off on The Great Migration." Zone 1 lights up in sepia amber around them. Suitcases at their feet. The "GO WEST" sign overhead. The chorus lands as they arrive: "I AM ALIVE IN LOS ANGELES."
06
Zone 2 · Jazz Era · the swing dance break
Poet pauses · band plays swingZone 2 lights up · golden washDancers swing · audience joins
Audience settles into Zone 2. Poet reaches the line "In the 1920s, South Central was singin…" — and pauses. The band lifts: swing music pours out. The five dancers break into a full swing-dance number in front of the Dunbar marquee. Mid-number, the dancers break the fourth wall and pull audience members in — partnered lindy, easy moves, laughter. A true communal moment. When the music resolves, the band drops to a soft bed and the poet resumes: "Jazz notes jumped down Central Ave… Duke Ellington played at Dunbar and Jack's Basket Room, too… All rhythm, no blues."
07
Transit · dancers lead east
Poet continuesAudience walks east
Poet threads through the walking material between zones. As dancers guide the audience toward Zone 3, she carries them through "60 years later, they presented the breaks…" setting up the next break.
08
Zone 3 · Birth of Hip Hop · the breakdance circle
Poet pauses · beat dropsZone 3 lights up · hard redRecord scratch · hip-hop drumsDancers form circle · audience rings them
Audience arrives in Zone 3. Poet hits "They presented the breaks…" — and pauses. Record scratch. A classic hip-hop beat drops hard from the band stage. The five dancers gather in the middle of the space, audience forming a circle around them. Breakdance cypher: top rock, down rock, power moves, freezes. Each dancer takes a turn in the middle. When the last freeze lands, the poet picks up: "Put a beat to their pain / Birthed hip-hop from heartache… World Class Wreckin Cru played at Eve's After Dark…" Poem continues as dancers reform into ushers.
09
Zone 4 · Leimert Park · the drum circle finale
Poet final stanzasZone 4 · deep green · Sankofa glowsLive congas from bandAudience gathers close to band stage
Dancers lead the audience to the center of the space, closest to the band stage — Zone 4, dressed as Leimert Park. The Sankofa symbol glows on the floor; the Leimert Park Village blade sign stands vertical at the edge; live congas kick in from the band. Poet: "Feel the rhythm of Leimert? The Village. The People… Sankofa symbols cover the pavement. 'Go back and get it.'" The poem's seven principles land here — "Unity, self-determination, collective work and responsibility, cooperative economics, purpose, creativity… Faith." Audience and dancers end gathered close around the drum circle.
10
Transition to Space 5
Tour Guide returnsExit east to Folk LA
Drum circle swells and slowly fades. Poet final lines. Tour Guide steps in and ushers the audience east toward Space 5 (Folk LA). Dancers remain at the Leimert Park zone, slowly slowing, until the last audience member passes through the east threshold.
Space 05 · Beat sequence

Folk LA

01
The invitation · the door · circle up
Tour Guide liveAudience knocks at the door · enters a homeShift to golden hour amber
As the Leimert Park drum circle fades, the Tour Guide pulls out his phone with a surprised little laugh, looks up, and turns to the audience: "I just got a text from a guy I met last week. He just asked if we want to come over, listen to some cool music… Very cool guy. He lives in the canyons. Plays guitar. Loves Joni Mitchell… what do you think? Should we go?" He leads the audience east — through the canyon — up to a wood-paneled front door with a stained-glass panel and a warm porch light above it. He knocks, and the door swings open. Inside, the Performer is already seated at the center of a cozy living room, acoustic guitar on his lap, quietly fingerpicking. Golden-hour amber. String lights twinkle overhead. The Tour Guide walks the audience straight into the center — past the couches and armchairs, onto the soft carpet — and gathers everyone close: "Come on in. This is somebody's living room. We're gonna do this the old way tonight — sit down, get comfortable, and just listen." He sits first, cross-legged on the carpet. The five dancers follow, each finding a spot around the Performer, forming a loose circle. The Tour Guide motions to the audience: "Join us. Sit on the floor, take a couch, lean on a friend — whatever feels right." Audience settles — many cross-legged on the carpet, some on couches and bean bags, the rest standing in the living room and canyon rings. The room quiets.
02
First section · "There was a moment in time" · first chorus
Performer plays + speaksSolo acoustic guitar
The Performer raises his eyes. The room is his. He opens with "There was a moment in time…" and plays through the full first section — Pete Seeger and the American folk movement, then into the Laurel Canyon naming (Neil, Joni, Eagles, Jim, Judy) — and lands the first full chorus. Last line hangs: "…no matter how many times it's heard."
03
The pause · at the record player
Performer walks to record playerCasual break · conversational
The Performer lets the silence sit. He sets his guitar down on the stool and rises — easy, unhurried. He walks to the record player, crouches, and starts flipping through the stack of records on the floor. He talks to the audience while he does — pulling one out, holding it up, remembering where he was the first time he heard it. Audience leans in. He glances at the band stage.
04
The invitation · "Hey band…"
Performer picks guitar back upBrief guitar figure, then silence
The Performer returns to his stool, picks up the guitar, and plays a brief phrase — just a few bars. Then stops. He cocks his head, smiles, and calls across the room toward the band stage in the SW corner: "Hey band. This feels too good to do alone. Y'all wanna come over to my place and jam?" A beat. Cheers and laughter from the band stage.
05
The band joins the circle
5-piece band crosses inBand picks up living room instruments · sits on floorWarmth spreads through whole room
The five band members step down off their stage, instruments in hand, and walk across the canyon ring into the living room. As they arrive, they see the instruments scattered around — the extra guitars on the stand, a tambourine on a side table, a harmonica on the coffee table, maracas on a couch, a hand drum by a bean bag. Each band member swaps or adds an instrument to what they already carry. They find spots on the carpet around the Performer — one beside the Tour Guide, two with the dancers, one leaning against a couch. It's not a concert anymore — it's a jam session in someone's home. The Performer nods. They all start playing together.
06
Canter's, Troubadour, Tower Records
Performer + band jammingRecord player picks up a spot
The group settles into a warm rolling groove. The Performer picks the thread back up: "There was a moment in time…" — and takes the audience through Canter's Deli, the Troubadour, Tower Records, and the lost ritual of sitting on the floor listening to a whole record end to end. The record player beside him catches a spotlight, platter slowly spinning, as he lands the section: "…until the record ends."
07
The psychedelic rabbit hole
Performer continuesString lights pulse color · amber → magenta → greenBand drifts into psychedelic texture
The band's groove opens out — dreamier, stranger. String lights overhead shift color slowly — amber bleeds into magenta, into green, back. The Performer guides the audience through the Leary-and-Alpert era — the Harvard psychedelic club, the rabbit hole, the guru, the frontier. He lands on the question: "Could the Harvard psychedelic club give birth to the summer of love without the drugs?"
08
Ash Grove · the roots of American music
Performer continuesAmber returns · single warm poolBand drops to rootsy quiet
Colors settle. Amber returns. The band drops into a quieter, rootsier feel — a blues walking line, brushed snare, plucked mandolin. The Performer invokes the Ash Grove — hillbilly ballads, gospel, blues, folk Black and White. He lands it with weight: "…when Ed Pearl dimmed the lights, the roots of American music came to life." A reverent pause. The room is still.
09
End of the era · Beach Boys to Zeppelin
Performer continuesGroove hardens · rock edges inCandles low · string lights dim
The band's groove hardens — electric guitar leaks in, drums get heavier. The mood tightens. The Performer opens with "The Beach Boys, surf rock, and the British Invasion — the inevitable end to the endless summer vacation…" and narrates the turn — times changing fast, Manson taking the innocence, psychedelics to speed, Elvis and Cash fading. He lands it on: "…the belting falsetto of Robert Plant."
10
Final chorus · "Folk is a seed" · audience engagement event
Performer + band + audience singFull room harmonyAudience engagement event · writer to define
The band eases back. The Performer takes the chorus again — open, warm, inviting: "Folk is a seed…" He turns the song outward. The dancers and the Tour Guide, still on the carpet, begin to hum the response line. [Audience engagement event — mechanic TBD by writer: could be hand-to-hand, circle-up, candle-pass, call-and-response hum, or movement-based sharing.] The whole room is in the song — a circle of people in someone's living room, singing together. The Performer lands the final line: "…singing together is the only medicine for the pain of one human voice." Held. The last chord rings.
11
Transition to Space 6 · the finale
Tour Guide stands · speaksExit NE corner · performer + band follow later
The chord fades. For a long breath nobody moves. Then the Tour Guide rises slowly from the carpet, takes a look around the room, and grins: "This was great. I could hang out here and make music all night. But sorry, [Performer] — we've got one more stop on our tour of Los Angeles." He motions for the audience to stand and follow. He leads them out through the NE corner of the space — toward the stage room. The Performer and band remain in the circle, quietly holding the room until the last audience member crosses the threshold — then they'll rise and follow for the finale.

The Stage

01
Arrival · finding seats
House warmAmbient low, sparseAudience loading
Audience exits Space 5, ramps down into the mid-house cross-aisle, and either descends toward R1–R3 or ascends toward R4–R8. The stage is empty. Black velour closed. The room deliberately shifts register from the immersive neighborhoods they've been walking through — for the first time tonight, they are sitting, and they are looking at a theater.
02
Tour guide solo
Single followspot · tight warmSolo voice · unaccompaniedTour Guide alone
House goes to black. A single followspot picks up the Tour Guide entering center stage through a gap in the velour. He stands alone on the warm walnut deck in front of the black drape. Solo opening — monologue, song, or hybrid (writer to define). The most intimate moment of the show: one person, one light, 150 people listening. Everything the production has been building toward arrives here.
03
Band arrives · sound layers in
Stage wash warms · individual specialsBass → drums → piano → hornsSL/SR wing entrances5-piece jazz band
One by one, the five band members enter from SL and SR wings. Bassist first — a quiet line under the tour guide. Then drums. Piano. Horns. Each instrument takes an upstage position as a special blooms. The stage picture expands from one to six; the sound gains shape around the solo voice.
04
Poets arrive · voices return
Individual specials on each speakerBand under · poets speak in sequenceAudience aisles · wings · through velour4–5 poets from earlier spaces
Poets from earlier spaces arrive one at a time via different paths — some through the audience aisles, some from the wings, one from upstage through the velour itself. Each gets a spoken moment — a line or fragment carried from their home space — before joining the picture. Language becomes the content. The audience hears voices they recognize from rooms they walked through an hour ago.
05
Dancers arrive · bodies fill the room
Full wash · movement pickups · velour edge-litRhythm forward · tempo upThrough the velourDance ensemble
The dancers arrive last. They enter through the velour — the first time anything has passed through the drape since the tour guide. Their bodies fill the space that words and music have been holding. Movement takes over from language. The band lifts into something more rhythmic. The stage is nearly full; the whole company is gathering.
06
Final number · velour opens · scrim ignites
Full stage · scrim back-lit · goldenFull band + voices · loudest of showVelour opens · scrim revealedFull company · ~20
The velour opens. Behind it, the white scrim lights from behind — deep, luminous, seemingly infinite. The stage suddenly has depth. Full ensemble plays, sings, moves. The biggest sound of the show. The biggest picture. This is the only moment in Space 6 where the design does something — everything else has been restraint. The reveal lands because nothing has been revealed before it.
07
Bow · no hierarchy
Hold on applause · slow fade · scrim lastOut · applause onlyFull company · single line
Full company spreads across the 45 ft stage in a single line — tour guide, poets, dancers, musicians — no hierarchy. One bow together. Lights hold for applause, then slowly fade. The scrim stays lit behind them until the last moment, then goes out with everything else. House up. The show is over. Company exits through velour gap; audience egresses via the rear walkway.
Space 07 · Beat sequence

The Real LA

01
Re-entry · the room remembered
Wall pre-lit · soft amber spillBand's last chord, slowly fadingAudience exits Space 6
The first audience members round the corner from Space 6 and re-enter the corridor. They recognize the room. Same proportions, same Walk of Fame stars on the floor, same massive west wall. For a half-second they expect the Hollywood content — the Sign, the cycling landmarks, the opening they walked in to. Instead, the wall is something else entirely. Recognition flickers into surprise. They slow down without realizing they've slowed down.
02
The skyline forms
First mosaic configuration · LA skylineLast chord still fading · ambient hush
The wall holds its first composition: the LA skyline, downtown towers and hills, rendered entirely from audience portraits arranged like mosaic tiles. From across the corridor it reads as a recognizable cityscape; up close, individual faces resolve. The text underneath, in slow Playfair italic: this is los angeles. It holds. The audience starts walking the length of the wall, scanning. Where am I?
03
The mural breathes
Reconfigure to beach · 30-sec holdReconfigure to freeway · 30-sec holdReconfigure to neighborhood · 30-sec hold
Every 30 seconds the mosaic reorganizes. The skyline dissolves; the same audience faces drift into a new composition — a beach with surfers, then a freeway at dusk with brake-light streams, then a residential neighborhood with palm trees and bungalows, then a mountain ridgeline. Each composition holds for 30 seconds before the next morph. The audience never sees the same image twice in a single egress window. The text underneath cycles too — sometimes this is los angeles, sometimes just los angeles, sometimes blank.
04
Finding yourself · photographing the wall
Audience pauses · phones up · selfiesNo performers · the audience is the show
The audience naturally arranges itself in two streams. Some walk the full length of the wall scanning for their face — they stop, point, laugh, photograph the wall, photograph themselves in front of it, show their friends. Others move along the east side of the corridor, taking it in but heading for the exit. The wall is photographable from every angle: the macro mural, the micro tile, the selfie. People linger. The corridor is full of soft phone-screen glow and quiet conversation. The Walk of Fame stars on the floor are dim, brass outlines only — they no longer demand attention.
05
Exit · turning out into the lobby
Wall continues · no fade-outBand's chord finally gone · ambient room toneMid-corridor turn · east wall doorway to lobby
The audience reaches the middle of the corridor and turns right through the doorway in the east wall — into the lobby and gift shop. The exit is mid-wall, not at the end, so the audience naturally walks past half the mural before turning out, then sees the south half of the mural as they pivot toward the doorway. Some keep walking south first to take in the full length of the wall before doubling back to the exit. The wall does not fade — it keeps cycling for the next person, and the next. The band's last chord has finally dissolved entirely; the room is silent except for the soft murmur of the audience itself. The lobby is calm and warmly lit. Front doors open onto the street. The audience steps out of the warehouse and back into the actual city of LA — the one the show has been telling them about. The closing image of the show is not on a screen. It is the city outside the door.
