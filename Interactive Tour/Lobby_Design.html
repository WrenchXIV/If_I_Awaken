If I Awaken in Los Angeles · Lobby & Front of House

The Lobby — outdoor courtyard meets indoor club

A two-part front-of-house experience. The audience first arrives in an outdoor courtyard with a Hollywood-style red carpet check-in under the LA sky, then steps through a single threshold door into a warm indoor club lounge with the bar, gift shop, and entrance to the show. Same ~9,500 sq ft of front-of-house, but split between sky and shelter.

Outdoor red carpet · Indoor lounge · Arts District industrial · Old Hollywood premiere

The lobby is split by an existing wall into two halves. The outdoor courtyard (~3,800 sq ft) sits at the north end — gated off the parking lot at its narrow tip, with the warehouse exterior wall on the west and a chamfered fence on the east. This is where the red carpet, the step-and-repeat backdrop, the photographer, and the ticket scan all live, in the open air. LA's weather makes this viable most of the year; a retractable canopy handles rain.

The indoor lounge (~5,700 sq ft) is the larger half, accessed through a single door in the middle of the green/blue wall. Inside: a ~64-ft bar, cocktail tables, a 5-booth VIP section along the angled wall, a curated gift shop split into north and south sections, and the show entry/exit doorway in the middle of the west wall (shared with Space 1 / Space 7). The aesthetic principle holds across both halves — Old Hollywood premiere glamour layered onto Arts District industrial — but expressed differently: the courtyard is sky-lit and palm-lined, the indoor is brick-and-velvet warm.

Pre-show, the audience flows parking → gate → red carpet → indoor door → bar → show. Post-show, they reverse: show → gift shop → optional bar → indoor door → courtyard → gate → parking. The single threshold door between the two zones means everyone passes through both spaces twice — once before the show, once after.

02Zone profiles
Three zones · creative direction + key elements
1 Outdoor Red Carpet · Check-in Outdoor green · ~3,800 sq ft · ~85-ft runway

Theme. "The Premiere — under the LA sky." A genuine Old Hollywood red carpet experience held outdoors in a courtyard, played with full theatrical commitment but a knowing wink — tonight, every guest is the celebrity, and tonight's premiere happens to be in the Arts District at golden hour. The audience's first encounter with the show is being greeted, photographed, and ushered as if they're arriving at a film premiere — but with the actual sky overhead, palm trees in the courtyard, and the warehouse exterior brick as the architectural frame.

The gate. A 21.7-ft-wide iron gate at the north end of the courtyard, opening directly off the parking lot. Above it, a small marquee with the show's name in script-style theatrical typography and frosted-bulb edge lighting. Industrial-iron pattern below; old-Hollywood marquee above. Same dramaturgical move as the lobby itself: glamour layered onto industrial.

The runway. A real red carpet, ~75 ft long and ~12 ft wide, runs flush against the west wall of the courtyard — the warehouse exterior brick. This positions the carpet along the longest straight wall of the courtyard, leaves the rest of the open courtyard available for the photographer, palms, tables, and circulation. The carpet ends about 12 ft shy of the indoor door; a short transition runner connects the end of the carpet to the doorway.

The backdrop. A 12-ft tall × 30-ft wide step-and-repeat backdrop is mounted directly on the same west wall (the warehouse exterior brick), positioned about halfway down the carpet so guests have already had a moment to register the experience before they pose. With the backdrop on the wall behind the carpet, the photo composition is natural: guest stands on the carpet, backdrop is the wall directly behind them, photographer captures from across the open courtyard. The backdrop is the show's logo and key art tiled in standard step-and-repeat grid, front-lit by warm theatrical fixtures.

The capture moment. The photographer (or calibrated camera rig) is positioned across the courtyard from the backdrop, about ~15 ft east of the carpet. A "host" in character welcomes each guest or group: "Step on the star, look this way, give us your best red-carpet smile." Photo is taken in 5–10 seconds. This becomes the input for the Space 7 Living Mosaic — the audience's enrollment moment.

The exchange. Tickets are scanned at the gate (just inside, by a host) so by the time guests reach the photo position they're already validated. After the photo, they continue down the carpet, step off onto the transition runner, and approach the indoor entry door. A coat check is positioned just inside that door.

Outdoor character. Concrete pavers underfoot. Velvet ropes on brass stanchions line the east side of the carpet (the warehouse exterior wall serves as the natural west boundary). Potted palms scattered along the eastern courtyard edge, opposite the carpet. Jacaranda trees if seasonal, succulents and native plants in low planters along the chamfered fence. String lights (Edison bulbs on warm-white) zig-zag overhead. A few small standing-height cocktail tables in the eastern half of the courtyard for early arrivals to wait outside before joining the carpet. Soft uplighting on the warehouse brick. The whole space reads as VIP entrance lane on the formal-architectural side, open courtyard on the social side.

Weather contingency. A retractable canopy or tensile shade structure covers the carpet and photo zone, deployable in 5 minutes if rain threatens. LA averages 35 rainy days/year, so this is the exception, but show-must-go-on requires it.

  • Materials: Red velvet carpet (weatherized) · brass stanchions · matte black backdrop with metallic gold logo print · iron gate with marquee · concrete pavers · planted palms + succulents
  • Lighting: Warm string lights overhead · theatrical front lights on backdrop · uplighting on warehouse brick · marquee bulbs · golden-hour-feel even after dark
  • Sound: Soft music spilling from indoor lounge through the door · jazz / Old Hollywood standards · ambient enough that you can still hear the city outside the gate
  • Throughput: 5 guests/minute = 30 minutes for 150 guests · two photo positions in parallel if needed
  • Staffing: 1 host at gate (ticket scan) · 1 host at carpet (in character) · 1 photographer · 1 floor manager
  • Weather plan: Retractable canopy over carpet + photo zone · backup plan to move to interior if storm
2 Indoor Bar & Lounge Indoor blue · ~3,700 sq ft (excl. gift shop) · 50-ft bar

Theme. "The Real LA Lounge." Crossing the indoor threshold from the outdoor courtyard is a transition from sky-lit premiere to warm interior club. The bar is the heart of the indoor lounge — somewhere off Sunset where industry people actually drink, where the playlist is right, where the photos on the walls are of real LA, and where the cocktails are named after places you'd be embarrassed not to know. Same dramaturgical principle as outside: glamorous on first glance; on closer inspection, the warehouse is still there.

The bar. A 50-ft bar runs along the south wall of the indoor lounge (the longest unbroken interior wall, 120.8 ft, gives ample room for the bar plus surrounding circulation). The backbar is a feature — full-height mirror, warm LED, brass-edged shelving stocked floor-to-ceiling with bottles. Velvet upholstered stools (16 along the bar). Three bartenders working — visible, theatrical, capable. A separate service well at one end for waitstaff to pull cocktails for the floor.

Floor seating. 6–8 cocktail tables scattered through the open floor — small (24" round), with 4 low velvet stools each. Plus a 5-booth VIP section along the angled NE wall (the room's outer chamfered corner) — each booth is 4-person with deep leather or velvet upholstery, a back bench against the wall, side benches, and a center table with a candle. The booths are spaced generously to give each group breathing room and privacy. The whole VIP zone is set apart with a velvet rope perimeter on brass stanchions and a subtle floor demarcation, with a dedicated host stand at its NW entry. The VIP designation is real (booths must be reserved or purchased separately) but the visual language stays warm and Old Hollywood — never bottle-service-club. Throughout the rest of the lounge: floor lamps, table candles, a few low brass pendants.

Walls. The existing red brick is left exposed — Arts District character preserved. Layered onto it: vintage LA neon signs (collected real signs from defunct LA businesses, or replicas — diners, motels, coffee shops, bars from East LA / Crenshaw / Koreatown). Framed B&W photos of Boyle Heights, Crenshaw, Koreatown, the Arts District itself in the 70s–90s — real LA, not Hollywood. One Spanish-tile accent panel as a feature wall. A small neon sign behind the bar reads simply "Los Angeles" in script.

Cocktail menu. Each drink named after a moment from the show or a real LA neighborhood. Examples: "Boyle Heights Sunset" · "The Topanga" · "Crenshaw & Slauson" · "The Sekou" (the Tour Guide's signature drink). Snacks lean into LA's culinary multiculturalism — elote cups, kalbi sliders, soul food bites, pupusas — sourced from real LA businesses listed on the menu.

Music. Pre-show playlist: jazz, Latin jazz, soul, R&B, funk — the musical lineage the band on stage is going to inherit. Curated by the show's composer. Volume conversation-friendly but unmistakably present. Spills out the indoor entry door into the courtyard so the courtyard isn't dead-quiet.

  • Materials: Polished concrete floor (existing) · brass fixtures · velvet upholstery (deep red, rust, ochre) · leather/velvet VIP booths (cognac) · velvet rope perimeter on brass stanchions · brass-edged glass · vintage neon · framed B&W photographs · Spanish tile accent panel · exposed red brick
  • Lighting: Warm (2700K) · backbar LED accent · table candles · sparse brass pendants · neon glow as the only color light
  • Capacity: 150 standing comfortably · ~52 seated (16 at bar + ~16 at cocktail tables + 20 in VIP booths) · room for circulation and ushers
  • Staffing: 3 bartenders · 2–3 cocktail servers · 1 host at indoor entry · 1 floor manager
3 Indoor Gift Shop · The City as a Store Indoor blue · ~1,348 sq ft total · split N + S with walking corridor

Theme. "The City as a Store." Not show merch. A curated LA general store — a love letter to the city's actual makers, writers, and small businesses, framed by the show as a way to take a piece of the experience home. The gift shop is where the show's argument extends past the wall: the real LA is people and culture, and here are some of the people and some of the culture.

Spatial split. The gift shop is divided into two adjacent sections along the indoor lounge's west wall, separated by a generous walking corridor that aligns precisely with the show entry/exit doorway. Post-show audience emerges from Space 7 directly into this corridor and is bracketed on both sides by retail. The split serves circulation: it preserves an unobstructed path from the bar/lounge to the show entry, and after the show, the audience can browse north then south (or vice versa) without ever feeling like the shop is in their way.

Gift Shop · NORTH — ~648 sq ft · 27 × 24 ft

  • Photo-print kiosk: Guests scan their souvenir card / QR code and print their best photos from the show on demand — Polaroid-style or postcard-size · digital web gallery available too. Positioned so the photo moment is the FIRST thing audience encounters after the Living Mosaic.
  • Show merchandise: Limited-edition program book · vinyl record of the show's score · screen-printed posters by LA artists (one per show space) · t-shirts in subtle script ("Los Angeles, 2026")

Gift Shop · SOUTH — ~700 sq ft · 28 × 25 ft

  • Books by/about LA voices: Joan Didion · Octavia Butler · Wanda Coleman · Sandra Cisneros · James Ellroy · Hector Tobar · Walter Mosley · Mike Davis · current poets and novelists rotated quarterly · a single curated reading list of ~30 titles
  • Goods from LA businesses: Coffee from Lincoln Heights · hot sauce from Boyle Heights · candles from a Black-owned LA brand · ceramics by an Asian-American LA potter · jewelry by local designers · prints by Topanga and Laurel Canyon artists · each item with a small card naming the maker and their neighborhood
  • Cashier & LA neighborhood feature wall: The map of LA with each product's neighborhood marked, by a commissioned LA artist. Final consolidation point before exit.

Visual. Mid-century retail aesthetic — wood shelving (oak or walnut), brass fixtures, soft warm lighting (3000K). The existing brick walls remain exposed. Wood + brick = warm, considered, anti-souvenir-shop. Display tables are low and angled so the room reads as inviting rather than dense. Both sections share the same materials palette so they read as one shop with a corridor through it, not two separate stores.

Walking corridor. ~17 ft wide between the two sections. The show entry/exit doorway sits in the corridor's western wall. Pre-show audience uses the corridor to walk west to the show entry; post-show audience emerges into the corridor and chooses N or S to browse. Width is intentionally generous — supports egress for 150 people in tandem with the bar zone egress paths.

  • Materials: Oak/walnut shelving · brass hardware · brick (existing) · soft warm lighting · printed cards naming each maker · LA-artist-made feature wall in south section
  • Lighting: Warm (3000K) · directional spots on featured products · ambient wash · gallery-like rather than retail-like · same fixtures in both sections for cohesion
  • Sound: Quiet · no overt music · ambient room tone bleeds in from the bar
  • Staffing: 1 retail associate per section (2 total) · 1 photo-print station operator in north
